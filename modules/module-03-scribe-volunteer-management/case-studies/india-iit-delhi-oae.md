# Case Study: IIT Delhi Office of Alumni and External Relations (OAE)

**Institution:** Indian Institute of Technology Delhi
**Location:** New Delhi, India
**Type:** Central Technical University (Institute of National Importance)
**Student Population:** 11,000+ (8,000 undergraduate, 3,000+ postgraduate)
**Students Served:** 130+ students with disabilities annually
**Scribe Programme Scale:** 200+ trained volunteers across 18 departments

## What They Built

IIT Delhi's Office of Alumni and External Relations operates India's most sophisticated department-level scribe panel system, serving as the institutional model for technical education accessibility. The programme maintains subject-specific volunteer pools across all engineering departments while ensuring academic integrity through systematic qualification verification and performance monitoring.

The **department-level coordination model** assigns 15-20 trained volunteers to each academic department, creating subject expertise pools that can handle specialized technical terminology while maintaining appropriate qualification differentials. Each department maintains year-level panels where third-year students serve second-year examinations, second-year students support first-year assessments, and specialized postgraduate panels assist PhD qualifying examinations.

**Technical innovation** includes the JAM (Joint Admission Test for MSc) scribe panel system, which provides scribes for national-level competitive examinations hosted at IIT Delhi. This system demonstrates scalable volunteer coordination for high-stakes examinations with stringent quality requirements and governmental compliance standards.

The programme integrates with IIT Delhi's broader **accessibility infrastructure** including accessible examination halls, assistive technology labs, and dedicated accommodation coordination that creates comprehensive support beyond scribe services alone.

## How It Works Operationally

### Recruitment and Training Framework

**Department-based recruitment** occurs through academic department orientation sessions where faculty members introduce scribe volunteering as skill-building opportunity complementing technical education. Each department maintains volunteer recruitment targets of 15-20 active scribes to ensure 2:1 volunteer-to-student ratios during peak examination periods.

**Training standardization** uses a centralized curriculum adapted for technical terminology and STEM-specific challenges. The OAE conducts monthly training workshops with department-specific modules covering mathematics transcription, engineering drawing description, and scientific notation standards. Training completion is tracked through departmental liaison faculty who verify volunteer competency and ongoing availability.

**Qualification verification protocols** ensure compliance with Government of India guidelines requiring scribes to have lower educational qualifications than examination takers. The system uses automated verification through student records integration, preventing inappropriate volunteer assignments while maintaining sufficient subject knowledge for technical terminology transcription.

### Sophisticated Scheduling and Matching

**The SPIDER system** (Student Programme for Inclusive Diversity and Educational Resources) manages volunteer scheduling through custom software that integrates student records, examination schedules, and volunteer availability data. The system automatically suggests optimal student-scribe pairings based on subject expertise, language preferences, availability patterns, and performance history.

**Three-tier backup protocols** ensure service reliability during high-demand periods. Primary volunteers commit to specific examinations 72 hours in advance, secondary volunteers maintain backup availability, and emergency pools provide last-resort coverage activated through automated SMS notification systems.

**Peak period management** during semester examinations requires coordination of 40-50 simultaneous scribe assignments across multiple campuses and examination venues. The OAE maintains central coordination with departmental liaisons to ensure comprehensive coverage while managing volunteer workload distribution and preventing burnout.

### Quality Assurance Innovation

**Performance tracking integration** uses post-examination feedback from students, volunteers, and examination administrators to create comprehensive service quality assessments. Data analysis identifies top-performing volunteers for recognition and training leadership roles while providing targeted improvement support for volunteers needing skill development.

**Technical accuracy monitoring** includes periodic review of mathematical and engineering transcriptions to ensure scientific notation correctness, equation formatting accuracy, and technical terminology consistency. Subject-matter faculty review sample transcriptions annually to validate transcription quality and identify training improvement needs.

**Student choice optimization** allows students to request preferred scribes based on positive previous experiences while maintaining programme flexibility for scheduling conflicts and volunteer availability changes. The system tracks student preferences and successful pairings to improve future matching algorithms.

## Scale and Impact

### Quantitative Outcomes

**Service volume statistics** demonstrate programme effectiveness: 130+ students with disabilities served annually across 450+ examination sessions, representing 15% growth in service utilization over three years. The programme maintains 95%+ volunteer attendance rates and 4.7/5 average student satisfaction scores across all academic disciplines.

**Academic performance correlation** shows students using scribe services achieve academic outcomes comparable to their peers without disabilities, with 92% course completion rates and average GPA within 0.2 points of department means. This data supports programme advocacy and demonstrates accommodation effectiveness to institutional stakeholders.

**Volunteer development impact** includes 78% of programme alumni reporting that scribe volunteering enhanced their professional skills, particularly technical communication, time management, and cross-cultural sensitivity. Many volunteers continue involvement in disability advocacy and accessibility professional roles after graduation.

### Institutional Integration Success

**Faculty partnership development** has created collaborative relationships with 85% of academic departments, with faculty members serving as departmental liaisons, providing training input, and advocating for programme resources. This integration ensures programme sustainability and institutional support during administrative transitions.

**Policy influence** extends beyond IIT Delhi, with the OAE model informing scribe programme development at other IITs and central technical universities. The programme's protocols have been adopted by 12+ similar institutions seeking to establish systematic scribe services.

**Research and evaluation components** include ongoing studies of accommodation effectiveness, volunteer skill development, and institutional accessibility culture that contribute to national disability inclusion policy development and higher education accessibility standards.

## What Makes It Distinctive

### Advanced Technology Integration

**Custom software solutions** integrate volunteer management with existing institutional systems including student information systems, examination scheduling platforms, and communication infrastructure. This integration reduces coordination burden while improving service reliability and data-driven decision making.

**Automated notification systems** use SMS and email to manage volunteer coordination, backup activation, and emergency response with minimal manual coordination required. Students receive automated confirmations and contact information while volunteers receive scheduling updates and training reminders through integrated communication platforms.

**Data analytics capabilities** enable programme evaluation through comprehensive performance metrics, trend analysis, and predictive modeling that inform resource allocation and programme improvement decisions. The system generates automated reports for institutional administration and governmental compliance requirements.

### Academic Excellence Integration

**Research participation opportunities** connect exceptional volunteers with faculty research projects related to accessibility, inclusive design, and assistive technology development. These opportunities provide career development while contributing to institutional research missions and accessibility innovation.

**Industry partnership development** leverages IIT Delhi's corporate relationships to create volunteer career networking opportunities and employment pathways that recognize scribe service as valuable professional experience. Partners include accessibility-focused technology companies and inclusive design consultancies.

**International collaboration** includes partnerships with overseas universities for volunteer exchange, training resource sharing, and comparative accessibility research that positions IIT Delhi as a global leader in technical education accessibility.

## Challenges and Limitations

### Resource and Sustainability Issues

**Coordination staffing demands** require 2.5 FTE staff dedicated to programme coordination, training, and quality assurance—a resource commitment that smaller institutions may struggle to replicate. Staff retention and professional development needs create ongoing human resources challenges.

**Technology infrastructure costs** for custom software development, system maintenance, and hardware requirements represent significant initial and ongoing investments that may not be feasible for institutions with limited technology budgets.

**Volunteer burnout prevention** remains challenging during peak examination periods when high-performing volunteers receive multiple service requests. Programme sustainability requires careful workload management and volunteer support systems that prevent overcommitment and service quality decline.

### Academic and Institutional Challenges

**Scalability limitations** constrain programme expansion as student demand grows faster than volunteer recruitment capacity. The programme has reached practical limits of department-based recruitment and requires alternative volunteer pipeline development to maintain service quality.

**Faculty engagement variability** across departments creates inconsistent support levels and volunteer quality differences that affect programme standardization efforts. Some departments provide excellent liaison support while others offer minimal engagement in recruitment and training activities.

**Government examination compliance** requires adherence to changing governmental guidelines that may conflict with institutional practices or volunteer availability. Balancing compliance requirements with programme effectiveness creates ongoing policy navigation challenges.

## Lessons Learned

### Implementation Success Factors

**Start with institutional commitment** demonstrated through dedicated staffing, adequate funding, and senior administrative support before launching comprehensive programming. Half-measures result in programme failures that damage institutional accessibility credibility.

**Technology serves coordination, not replacement** of human relationship building between students, volunteers, and programme staff. Automated systems improve efficiency but cannot substitute for personal attention and community building that sustain volunteer engagement.

**Quality over quantity** in volunteer recruitment ensures programme reputation and service reliability that enable sustainable growth. Recruiting fewer well-trained committed volunteers produces better outcomes than large pools of poorly prepared participants.

### Strategic Development Recommendations

**Phase implementation gradually** from small-scale pilot programmes to comprehensive institutional systems, learning from each expansion phase before attempting full-scale operation. Rapid scaling often compromises quality and threatens programme sustainability.

**Invest in coordinator professional development** through specialized training in disability services, volunteer management, and programme evaluation. Coordinator competency directly influences programme effectiveness and institutional support levels.

**Build multiple funding streams** combining institutional budget allocation, government scheme participation, and external partnership support to ensure programme continuity during budget constraints or administrative changes.

## Replication Guidance

### For Similar Technical Institutions

**Adapt department-level coordination** to institutional academic structure and scale, maintaining subject expertise benefits while simplifying coordination complexity for smaller programmes. Focus on core STEM departments initially before expanding to specialized programmes.

**Develop technology solutions** appropriate to institutional capacity and resources, beginning with simple coordination tools before investing in comprehensive automated systems. Excel-based tracking may be adequate for programmes serving fewer than 50 students annually.

**Establish faculty partnerships** early in programme development to ensure academic integration and ongoing institutional support. Faculty champions provide programme advocacy and volunteer recruitment support that external coordination cannot replicate.

### For General University Application

**Extract coordination principles** rather than specific technical implementations: systematic training, department-level recruitment, performance monitoring, and student choice optimization apply across academic disciplines and institutional types.

**Scale volunteer management** approaches to institutional size and resources while maintaining quality assurance and volunteer support standards. Small programmes need simplified versions of IIT Delhi's sophisticated coordination systems.

**Maintain governmental compliance** requirements through documentation systems and training standards appropriate to institutional examination practices and regulatory oversight responsibilities.
